SUMMARY
This is the prototype of a potential mobile app for home security. The entire concept and design of the app is a project assignment, part of the CALART’s online course “UX Design Fundamentals”, on Coursera.

The Client

Peekaboo wants to join the home surveillance market as the most affordable, versatile and easy to use monitoring system.

They want to build a mobile app that offers a more casual alternative to traditional home security systems and their key features are simplicity, mobility, and affordability.

The Concept

Have you ever been away from home, but wondered what your pet is up to? You can easily find this out with Peekaboo.

Peekaboo is a paired mobile app that, on one hand, will enable your mobile device’s video camera to sense movement and on the other hand, allow you to see what is happening in its range, from a remote location. Its notification service works economically in your favour, taking and sending you photos only when movement is sensed. But you can also choose to connect remotely from the paired device, whenever you want, and watch a live stream video.

It can be installed on any Android/ IOS / Windows Phone 10 (or higher) mobile device.

It is ideal for pet monitoring but it also provides the most affordable home surveillance system.

The Competition

There are apps and systems on the market that check some of the functionalities of Peekaboo, but not all of them at once.

For example, Skype has an auto-answer option, so you can video call anytime from another mobile device and this way you create a remote connection and can see the live video stream through the camera of the monitoring device. But it doesn’t have a motion detection notification system. And you also require two different login accounts on each of the two devices.

TeamViewer provides full remote control from another device, but the pairing between the devices must be done each time with a different password, automatically generated by the monitoring device. So unless this password is provided to the receiving device, the remote connection cannot be established.

Alfred Systems Inc is the main competitor. This app has almost all of the functionalities provided by Peekaboo: motion detection notification system with photos, remote connection, management of received photos and notifications, an easy pairing mechanism, using only the user’s Gmail address which is entered on both devices during setup. However, this exact simplicity prevents it from allowing multiple devices to be paired to the monitoring one.
Peekaboo addresses this limitation and makes it possible, in an easy way, to pair multiple devices at the same time. This way, all of the devices will be notified at once when motion is sensed.

Target Audience

The idea comes mainly in support of the people who have pets and travel but want to check in on them and be assured that they are ok.

Other categories of people could find different benefits from using the app as well:
- Cautious people that are on a budget will find the app to be an economical surveillance system, from a financial perspective;
- Because the app works only when there’s movement, this will make it attractive to environmental conscientious people who want to have a surveillance system that is economical from an energy-saving perspective;
- The app can also come to the aid of practical people, with small children as they can monitor them from a different room of the house.

Organising the information

In order to articulate and structure the goals of the app, I first identified the correct functional requirements, that respond directly to the user and client needs.

The app has to work interchangeably as a monitoring tool and as a remote access point.

As a monitoring tool:
- The app provides a setup through which the user creates the connections between the current device and the ones that will later receive the notifications. This is made possible based on a uniquely generated key that is shared with each paired device.
- The app will then make use of the mobile device’s camera to take photos whenever it senses movement and send them as push notifications to the paired devices.
- The setup will also allow the user to choose the frequency of the notifications, the camera sensitivity or whether to use the flashlight or not.

As a remote access point:
- the user can establish a remote connection from the paired device towards the monitoring device, at any time.
- It saves and provides a medium for managing the photos.

Final Product

SPLASH SCREEN

The splash screen is a short animation where the product introduces itself by presenting its logo and tagline in a ludic way. This sets the tone for its lighthearted ease of use.

At the end of the animation, there’s a call for action which lets you know that a setup procedure is in order next. But rest assured, this is accomplished through a common effort of both yourself, as the user, and the product, which will guide you properly along the way.

HOME PAGE

After login, the home screen presents the two complementary options for how the app can be used. The labels for these options were chosen to be relatable and recognisable with each of their deed.

The tutorial jumps in to explain in a few more - but still simple - words, each of the two options, intentionally avoiding any words with technical connotations.

1. THE MONITOR

The first option is the one through which the monitoring can be done. The lower menu gives you the setup areas: linked devices ("Watchers"), notifications and camera, each properly introduced by the tutorial in sequential order.

Watchers (Linked Devices)

This is an important step in the setup. It’s how the linking with other devices is done. Additional tutorial info is provided along the way, for successfully carrying out the task.

To faster the connection, options for sharing the pairing key with the corresponding paired device are provided via various social media environments.

Notifications, Camera & Monitor

Further setup options are available for notification type and frequency and also for camera sensitivity, flash and remote connection.

The monitoring can start at any time and since it’s the main action of this section, its button was granted the most screen real estate.

2. THE WATCHER

The second option of the Peekaboo app enables the remote connection (“Live peek”) and has a system for notification management, where the user can view, filter or delete the pictures received.

It's the other half of the pair, complementary to the first one. To sustain this duality, the colours have been reversed: the header is now orange, the bottom menu is gray. To enhance the correlation even more, the icon for the remote connection is the exact same one used for the "Watchers", only enlarged to take the most real estate, as this is the main action in this section of the app.

The Progress

The concept and design of the app were completed in 1 month. The final version of the product includes the feedback of three unmoderated user test sessions, held on the Coursera platform. After each unmoderated test session, I received feedback from 3 users.

The first session revealed that the terminology used for labelling was considered either too abstract or too technicall. The initial labels can be seen in the wireframes, and words like “sender”, “receiver” or "devices" were not appealing to the users, some even considering them confusing. This set me up to find more practical and graspable terms for the technical setup of the app.

After the second test session, improvements were made also on the UI part. The users pointed out that some of the colours were too desaturated and that the appearance transgressed into muddy. Therefore, I chose brighter, but still warm colours that are not too striking.

Then

Now

The third and final test session confirmed that the changes brought noticeable improvements. Positive feedback was received on the app’s intuitiveness and ease of use. And after the adjustment of the colour palette, the current look and feel was appreciated as “cute and cosy”.

Conclusion

Working on this prototype, I learned more about the importance of labelling as part of the information architecture. Any chosen name should correctly embody the information it represents.
On the other hand, the choice of colours as part of the UI, should back up the visual harmony of the interface.
